A Wine Day Trip from Paris

My super fun Burgundian adventure with Rache

late June 2026
Saint-Bris · Coulanges-la-Vineuse · Chablis
scroll

For the day we were in the hands of the knowledgeable and convivial Luis André of Wine Tours Paris. We set off from Paris under a mild, blue sky — a merciful break from the epic heatwave of the week before, and just about perfect for a day of touring Burgundy — and pointed southeast into the older, quieter Burgundy of the Yonne: the part most people drive past on their way to somewhere more famous, and shouldn’t.

Paris → Saint-Bris → Coulanges-la-Vineuse → Chablis → home.
Stop One

Saint-Bris — nine centuries underground

Saint-Bris-le-Vineux

Our first stop was a medieval village where the church goes back to the 12th century and the houses average four hundred years old. Beneath it all, cellars dug in the 1100s. Burgundy, it turns out, spent the Hundred Years’ War allied with England against France — and Saint-Bris was a fortress on the road to Dijon. History tastes different down there.

At Domaine Bersan we went down into those cellars: a low brick-vaulted tunnel like a small cathedral, oak barrels running off into the dark under their pale chalk bungs, the ceilings feathered with a soft black mould that turns out to be entirely benign — it feeds on the wine vapour and slowly oxidises the limestone. In one corner, a bin of ancient, dust-furred bottles with a stoneware jug keeping watch. In another, a passage so narrow you had to duck and turn sideways.

❦Rachel threaded through it like she’d lived there nine hundred years.

Then, the wines: four whites — a crisp village Chablis, a Côte d’Auxerre, the local oddity “Mont Embrasé” (Saint-Bris is the one corner of Burgundy that grows Sauvignon Blanc), up to a Montmains Premier Cru — then the reds, an Irancy “Vau Falleau” and a Côte d’Auxerre “Les Hourdons.” We’d had our eye on a particular Chablis to take home; they’d sold out, so a bottle of Bersan’s “Les Ouches” Chablis — light, clean, a happy understudy — rode back to Paris with us.

Stop Two

Coulanges-la-Vineuse — the King’s vineyard, and a library

Domaine du Clos du Roi

This was the heart of the day. The estate is called Clos du Roi — “the King’s vineyard” — for a plot that once supplied the crown after the war. It’s run by Magali Bernard, who names her cuvées after her daughter Coline and her son Charly.

We ate lunch there, simply and perfectly: a charcuterie board — a delicious hard, almost-salumi sausage, cheese, ham, good bread — alongside the “Charly,” with a Burgundy map for a tablecloth and a cheeky drinkers’ creed pinned to the wall. Then the surprise of the day: a caramel ice cream paired with the Ratafia de Bourgogne, a ruby, 100%-Pinot-Noir fortified wine we’d never even heard of — traditionally poured with dessert, and a revelation against the caramel.

And — of course — in a village this small, Rachel still managed to find the bibliothèque, and stood under its hand-lettered sign looking thoroughly pleased with herself.

Stop Three

Chablis — the grown-up finale

Domaine Long-Depaquit · Albert Bichot

We finished in Chablis itself. First, a walk in the vines: a slope of still-green Chardonnay, the clusters hard little marbles weeks from ripe, looking out over the whole valley. The summer’s heat — that epic heatwave the week before — had Luis predicting an early harvest: nothing certain yet, but very likely, with Chablis now coming in around late August where a decade ago it waited for mid-September.

Then the formal tasting at Domaine Long-Depaquit (part of the venerable Albert Bichot, est. 1831) — the proper grown-up one, in a room hung with parcel maps of the very slopes in our glasses. It climbed from a fresh, steel-fermented village Chablis through the Premier Crus and into the Grand Crus — Les Vaudésirs, Bougros — before ending on La Moutonne, the tiny near-mythical monopole.

❦It doesn’t shout. It just announces itself on the finish.

Afterward we wandered the town: the river Serein threading green between the stone houses, flowers spilling off the bridge, a blackbird on a chimney, the town’s old coat of arms — royal fleurs-de-lis on one side, a little gold chariot on the other. And Rachel, two for two on the day’s discoveries, bought some mustard.

Home

By the time we rolled back into Paris, we’d gone from Roman grapes and escape tunnels to grand cru Chablis to a village library — all in a single, sun-baked, slightly tipsy day. The souvenirs: a bottle of Chablis and a jar of mustard. Northern Burgundy, it turns out, gives you the whole story — if you let it.
